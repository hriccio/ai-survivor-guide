## Introduction {#introduction}

You do not need to become an AI expert. You need enough literacy to make conscious decisions in a world shaped by AI.

This manual is a starting point. It organizes simple principles for thinking about work, automation, learning, and human relevance without depending on hype, fear, or instant productivity promises.

## Chapter 1: AI changes the cost of producing answers {#chapter-1}

![A warm desk with a laptop and many abstract answer cards.](assets/images/chapters/01-answers-generated.png)

AI lowers the cost of generating text, images, code, plans, and variations. That does not mean every generated answer is correct, useful, or ethical.

The new problem is not only producing more. The new problem is choosing better: which question to ask, which answer to trust, which risk to accept, and which consequence to own.

## Chapter 2: Automation does not remove context {#chapter-2}

![A cozy workspace where workflow cards sit beside maps and calendars.](assets/images/chapters/02-context.svg)

Tools automate parts of work, but they do not carry the human, social, cultural, and economic context where a decision happens.

The more a task affects people, money, reputation, or safety, the more important it is to understand the context before delegating.

## Chapter 3: Human relevance depends on judgment {#chapter-3}

![A calm desk with option cards and a balance scale.](assets/images/chapters/03-judgment.svg)

Judgment is the ability to compare options, notice trade-offs, and take responsibility for a choice.

In an era of abundant answers, relevant people are not only the ones who can operate tools. They are the ones who can interpret situations, define criteria, and decide clearly.

## Chapter 4: Learning became continuous practice {#chapter-4}

![A bright study corner with notebooks and a circular path of study cards.](assets/images/chapters/04-learning.svg)

Learning about AI does not mean tracking every new product. It means keeping a stable enough mental model to evaluate change without panic.

A good question is worth more than an endless list of tools.
